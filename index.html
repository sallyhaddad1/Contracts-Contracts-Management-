Objective of Contract Management

Contract management integrates the processes used to manage contracts throughout the contract life cycle while ensuring customer satisfaction. It includes job tasks involved in managing deliverables, deadlines, and contract terms and conditions while ensuring customer satisfaction.

Contract Life Cycle

- Pre Award – Develop Solicitation – Solicitation Develop Offer - Offer
- Award – Form Contract - Contract
- Post Award – Perform Contract –Contract Performance Close Contract – Closes Contract

Definitions

Contract management is the process of managing contract creation, execution, and analysis to maximize operational and financial performance at an organization, all while reducing financial risk.
A contract is a mutually binding, legal relationship that obligates the seller to furnish supplies or services and the buyer to provide consideration for them.

Essential Elements of a Contract

** In order for a contract to be valid, it must contain four essential elements:
 - Demonstration of mutual agreement through
- (1) an offer and (2) acceptance
- (3) Consideration (i.e., something of value—typically money), and
- (4) Intent to create a legal relationship.
** The legal relationship is demonstrated through the contract’s:
 - Legal purpose (a contract for an illegal act is not valid), and
- Capacity of the parties (the contracting parties must have the legal
capacity to enter into a contract).

Types of Contracts

Bilateral Contract Firm-fixed-price contracts, as the name implies, establish a price that is not subject to any adjustment based on the contractor’s actual cost to perform the contract.
Unilateral Contract Only one party has made a promise and is obligated to perform. An example of this may be a contract that promises a monetary reward to find a missing pet. No one is obligated by that contract to search for that pet, but if the pet is found, the party who promised the reward is obligated to pay.
Express Contract The contract terms are stated in either written or spoken words and are assented to by both parties.
Implied Contract Sometimes called “implied in fact,” this contract is one in which the contract terms are wholly or partly inferred from conduct or surrounding circumstance.
Quasi Contract Sometimes called “implied in law,” this contract comprises obligations imposed by law to prevent the unjust enrichment of one person at another’s expense.

Contract Types

Fixed-price contracts Most contracts are bilateral, meaning they consist of reciprocal promises. Under bilateral contracts, each party has made a promise, has an obligation to perform, and has expectations that the other party will perform. An example is a contract where one party promises to provide services and the other party promises to pay an agreed-upon amount of money to receive those services.
Cost-reimbursement contracts Only one party has made a promise and is obligated to perform. An example of this may be a contract that promises a monetary reward to find a missing pet. No one is obligated by that contract to search for that pet, but if the pet is found, the party who promised the reward is obligated to pay.
Incentive contracts Incentive contracts, contracts containing various forms of incentives, may be appropriate when there is a desire or need to provide a contractor/ seller with additional motivation to attain specific acquisition objectives that would be unlikely to be attained without the incentives.
Other contract types
Time-and-Materials (T&M) Contracts
T&M contracts are used to buy goods or services based on direct labor hours and the cost of materials required for contract performance.

Pre-Award Life Cycle Phase

Pre-Award is the first phase of the contract life cycle and includes the processes for buyers to produce solicitations and sellers to prepare offers. The pre-award life cycle consists of two domains that produce significant contract management outcomes:

Develop Solicitation The process for the buyer of determining customer requirements and then describing and explaining the requirements and other relevant terms and conditions through a solicitation to the sellers.
Develop Offer The process for the seller of developing business practices and strategies to respond to solicitations with the intent of winning contracts and meeting performance requirements.

Award Life Cycle Phase

- The second phase of the contract life cycle is Award. The award process involves all the work by both the buyer and seller that produces an awarded contract.
- There is one domain in the award phase: Form Contract. The job tasks and competencies of the Form Contract domain produce the contract.

Post-Award Life Cycle Phase

Perform contract is the process of establishing and maintaining communications and tracking and documenting contract performance. The value added by this process is ensuring compliance with contractual terms and conditions during contract performance through contract closeout or termination.

Perform contract is composed of four competencies:
1. Administer contract
2. Ensure quality
3. Subcontract management
4. Manage changes

Important elements of Administer Contract

1. Using effective communication
2. Attending the post-award kickoff meeting
3. Conducting status review meetings
4. Preparing written status reports
5. Observing and monitoring performance
6. Documenting performance and providing feedback to the seller, and
7. Resolving requests for equitable adjustment and claims.

Subcontract Management

is the process of contract management of subcontracts, with the prime contractor being responsible for the performance of its supply chain.

Manage Changes

After contract award, buyers and sellers frequently discover contract changes are necessary. Changes must be managed to ensure configuration control of the contract and subsequent contract performance. A contract change is necessary when the existing contract does not reflect what needs to be accomplished. Such contract changes may be necessary due to changed or unforeseen circumstances, conduct of the parties, and/or changes in buyer requirements, including funding limitations.

Contract Closeout

Contract closeout is the process of ensuring all performance has been accomplished, final payment has been made, and the contract has been reconciled.

These required tasks normally include:
1. Verifying all required goods or services have been received and accepted;
2. Verifying all contractor invoices have been received and paid;
3. Returning or disposing of any buyer-furnished property;
4. Closing subcontracts by the seller;
5. Agreeing that no claims, issues, or unresolved matters exist;
6. Obtaining a final report of patents and royalties;
7. Signing a formal notice of contract completion;

Guarantees/Bonds

1. Advance Bank Guarantee/ Down Payment Guarantee;
Under an advance payment bank guarantee, the guarantor undertakes to repay an advanced payment that the principal has received in the event that the principal does not fulfill the terms of its contract.

2. Performance Bank Guarantee;
Performance guarantee issued by a bank and protects the beneficiary against the failure of the principal to meet its contractual obligations in the underlying contract.

3. Retention Money Guarantee (could be replaced with retention amount)
Retention Money Guarantee is a written document issued to the owner or the buyer from the Bank to guarantee that applicant will continue to fulfill contract obligation after withdrawing final payment of the contract price in advance, at the request of construction contractor or supplier.

4. Parent Company Guarantee (an undertaking by Contractor’s Parent Company) ;
A parent company guarantee is typically a promise by the parent company to stand behind the contractor and to be responsible for the contractor’s default of its contractual obligations. Alternatively, it may be limited to an obligation to step in and perform the contractor's obligations. In either case, the parent company's liability may be capped.

Insurances/Indemnifications

1. Workmen’s compensation/Employers Liability;
Covers statutory liability to employees for work related death, injury or illness.

2. Motor vehicle third party and passenger liability;
It covers only your legal liability for the damage you may cause to a third party – bodily injury, death and damage to third party property – while using your vehicle

3. Contractor Plant & Equipment;
The cover is designed for Mobile Plant & Equipment used in construction sites against sudden, accidental and unforeseen loss or damage but excluding mechanical/electrical breakdown. The same criteria apply as in the case of Machinery Insurance.

4. General Third Party Insurance;
 General liability insurance helps pay expenses related to third- party injuries and property damage.

5. VESSEL hull and machinery insurance, (if applicable);
6. Professional indemnity;
 Professional Indemnity insurance is to cover your legal liability for the advice and design that you provide and also ensures protection against liability for breach of professional duty that you may face when providing a service.

7. Contractor All Risk (CAR) and/or Erection All Risk (EAR);
 The policy has two Sections to cater for all contingencies associated with Contract works.
- Section I : Material Damage This Section covers loss or damage to contract works and other ancillary facilities on comprehensive basis
- Section II : Third Party Liability This Section covers legal liabilities to third parties arising out of accidental bodily injury or property damage in connection with contract works.
